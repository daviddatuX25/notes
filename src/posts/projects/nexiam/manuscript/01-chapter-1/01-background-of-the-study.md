Chapter 1

INTRODUCTION

Background of the Study

Assessment serves two purposes. The first is validation which gives feedback to students on what they have learned. The second is academic filtering that tests whether learners can excel in a certain field, or in this case, whether they are aligned with the university course they intend to pursue. Assessments remain prevalent in higher education. A 2024 scoping review by French et al. found that although assessments offer pedagogical benefits like stronger knowledge retention and increased motivation for some learners, these benefits are often outweighed by drawbacks such as heightened student anxiety, surface-level learning, and equity concerns. This means assessment design continues to receive scrutiny, including the psychometric properties of examinations. However, besides psychometric issues, there is also a gap concerning the operational logistics of administering examinations at scale. This operational perspective is comparatively under-researched, especially from the standpoint of institutional impact (Woelert, 2023).

This revelation along with the researchers' background sets the direction of this study to solve the bottlenecks on the non-psychometric side of admissions, specifically the infrastructure surrounding the admission process. Patil et al. (2021) found that large-scale examinations and admissions encounter challenges with record integrity, applicant verification, and resource coordination, particularly when paper-based workflows are used. Sánchez-Mendiola et al. (2025) demonstrated such challenge when analyzing results from over 600,000 examinees on one large-scale exam. In contrast, Thakur et al. (2025), through their TrueGradeAI framework, showed that digital admission pipelines do more than automate scoring. They introduce architectural patterns that reduce grading bias and human subjectivity. The point is that while psychometric properties get considerable attention, the operational side of admissions remains under-invested despite being the component responsible for administering examinations. As a result, institutions often lack the downstream structures needed to process scores and deliver results efficiently.

Liu et al. (2025) further explained that conventional admissions still rely on fixed historical yield assumptions while overlooking applicant behavior uncertainty. This means prolonged decision timelines distort enrollment projections and make it harder for institutions to convert admissions into enrollment. Esquivel and Esquivel (2020) demonstrated how predictive modeling is used to estimate class sizes from an already admitted but unenrolled students in a Philippine university. This weighs heavy for an institution's resource allocation and projections. After results are released, speed matters from the applicant's perspective because many applicants receive multiple offers and will commit to whichever institution processes them faster (Ruffalo Noel Levitz, 2023). Slow admissions are not merely an inconvenience. They are structural barriers. For a public state college on a fixed enrollment calendar, every day spent through inefficient scoring and various delays, is a day where potential applicants could consider another institution, especially in the perspective and bias of efficient service delivery. This necessitates a pipeline that is fast on top of an effective delivery.

In the global landscape, admission systems have also moved toward innovation. Ever since the COVID-19 pandemic, many institutions have fast tracked their adoption of artificial intelligence and cloud platforms to modernize administrative services (EDUCAUSE, 2023; Marinoni et al., 2020). Likewise, the application of machine learning to admission processes is also becoming more relevant. Lira et al. (2023), publishing in Science Advances, found that machine learning models can evaluate applicants using unstructured application materials like essays, and that these assessments correlate with long-term life outcomes. This strengthens the case for AI within admissions. However, Van Busum and Fang (2025) examined bias in AI admission models and raised fairness concerns about algorithm-assisted decision-making. Therefore, AI should not completely replace human judgment but instead function as part of a hybrid model where professional judgment remains central.

With regards to unified admission platforms, Adwan et al. (2023) developed a centralized mobile-based admission application for universities in the GCC region. Their system achieved a 97.7% usability using Nielsen heuristics, which shows that unified admission platforms are practical across institutions. Inferring from this, a unified admission pipeline combines several operational features into one platform to address such admission-related bottlenecks. One onset feature is AI-powered chatbots, which is shown to handle routine applicant questions consistently at scale (Labadze et al., 2023). Another feature is role-based access control, which is important for keeping educational data secure at an institutional scale (Sandhu et al., 1996; Kabier et al., 2023).

Nationally, the Philippines' digitalization has been driven more by policy rather than by market pressure. The CHED Strategic Plan (CHED, 2022) sets such institutional direction. Rooting from the Republic Act No. 10931 (2017) which increased admission volumes after removing tuition fees in public higher education, putting more pressure on staff and infrastructure. Alongside Republic Act No. 10844 (2016) which established the DICT, the pressure makes it more real as it makes ICT policy a government-wide mandate. In hindsight, the Republic Act No. 10173 (2012) adds to this pressure the requirement to secure handling of applicant records, which paper systems cannot fully support. These laws make digitization both necessary and challenging. The National Privacy Commission (2020) has issued advisories for the education sector on handling applicant records. On top of this, Villarino (2025) found that students in provincial state colleges already use consumer AI tools ahead of their own institutions. Ilagan et al. (2025) developed a virtual support agent for Filipino university students, showing that LLM-based student services are deployable in the Philippine HEI context. This creates a governance gap that bypasses proper compliance with Republic Act No. 10173, despite students being ready and AI-literate. Admissions should therefore take advantage of this familiarity by using students' AI literacy into institutional applications instead of leaving it under-utilized. AI chatbots are one application of how this can work.

Another relevant point is that every feature within a unified admission pipeline should first be validated independently. Cuerdo et al. (2021) validated EvalBee, an optical mark recognition tool, in a Philippine public school. Earlier, Catalan (2017) demonstrated automated multiple-choice scoring using readily available software at De La Salle University, tested on 800 answer sheets across eight courses. Dayata and Yap (2024) deployed a retrieval-augmented generation pipeline at the University of San Carlos, published at IEEE COMNETSAT. Tan (2025) validated data-driven optimization through K-Means algorithm for student course selection at an ACM conference. Mittal and Malhotra (2026) confirmed through a comprehensive survey that clustering-based recommendation approaches remain a staple in this domain. Together, these independent solutions support the development of an integrated admission pipeline.

Locally, ISPSC Tagudin reflects these national issues. The campus processes about 700 to 1,000 freshman applicants every year using a fully manual workflow covering intake, examination, and result delivery. Previous campus studies have tried to improve institutional processes, but none addressed admissions directly. Yukee et al. (2025) validated K-Means profiling on the campus's own entrance exam data. Ballesteros et al. (2025) confirmed that K-Means works for student profiling at ISPSC. Eustaquio and Nisperos (2023) built a web-based registration system with decision-support for billing, grading, scholarship, and retention tracking.

These studies show that data-driven decision-support systems already exist on campus. Eustaquio and Nisperos in particular demonstrate that a single digital platform can support multiple administrative functions, providing a conceptual basis for an integrated admission platform. However, their system focused on post-admission processes, not the admission testing phase itself. Angala et al. (2023) implemented a document management system, and Cenezan and Gacusan (2025) built analytics-driven records management.

The campus does not lack successful digitization projects or local research. What it lacks is a unified admission testing system, one platform that can scale and be architecturally integrated, bringing together scoring, aptitude assessment, applicant classification, and structured guidance within one pipeline. As presented in various studies, these pieces are scattered. Yukee's clustering work is deployed but lacks features for admission pipeline inclusion. Cuerdo's OMR validation sits in a journal. Dayata's chatbot is proven to work and is ready for replication. All these features, altogether, have never been connected into one pipeline that is proven to work on an admission process and in the context of how Guidance and Registrar can manage these during an actual admission season. Dash et al. (2026) proposed a similar AI-assisted unified platform for college applications but stopped at the design stage, confirming that the gap is in deployment and governance, not in concept. This integration has to comply with Republic Act No. 10173 by securing both physical records and digitally processed applicant information, especially when data is processed externally (Slade and Prinsloo, 2013). For the AI component, retrieval-augmented generation should draw from institutional policy documents rather than unrestricted language models to reduce hallucinations (Lewis et al., 2020).

Therefore, a unified, role-governed admission testing system is the gap that surfaces from these literature reviews and contemporary necessities. A platform that can integrate applicant registration, streamlined testing operations, automated scoring, decision-support with AI advisory services, and result delivery into one admission ecosystem serving the Guidance Office, Registrar, and other institutional stakeholders. Finally, this needs-finding surfaces two fundamental questions: whether a unified platform outperforms existing traditional processes, and whether it is acceptable according to its intended purpose across different users and use cases.
