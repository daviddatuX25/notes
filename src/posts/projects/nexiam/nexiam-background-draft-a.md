Assessment has two purposes. The first is validation, which provides feedback to students. The second is academic filtering, which tests whether learners can excel in a certain field or, in this case, whether they are aligned with the university course they intend to pursue. Assessments continue to be prevalent in higher education. A 2024 scoping review by French et al. found that although assessments provide pedagogical benefits such as stronger knowledge retention and increased motivation for some learners, these benefits are often outweighed by drawbacks including heightened student anxiety, surface-level learning, and equity concerns. This means that assessment itself continues to receive scrutiny, including the psychometric properties of examinations or testing. Besides psychometric issues, there is also a gap regarding the operational logistics of administering examinations at scale. This operational perspective is comparatively under-researched, especially from the perspective of institutional impact (Woelert, 2023).

The visible direction of this study is therefore to examine the bottlenecks on the non-psychometric side of admissions or testing, specifically the infrastructure surrounding the admission process. Patil et al. (2021) found that large-scale examinations and admissions encounter challenges involving record integrity, applicant verification, and resource coordination, particularly when traditional paper-based workflows are used. In contrast, Thakur et al. (2025), through their TrueGradeAI framework, demonstrated that digital admission pipelines do more than automate scoring. They introduce architectural patterns that reduce grading bias and human subjectivity. The argument here is that while psychometric properties receive considerable attention, the operational side of admissions remains under-invested despite being the component responsible for administering examinations. As a result, institutions often lack the downstream structures needed to process scores and release results efficiently. Liu et al. (2025) further explained that conventional admissions still rely on fixed historical yield assumptions while overlooking applicant behavior uncertainty. This means prolonged decision timelines distort enrollment projections and hinder institutions in effectively converting admissions into enrollment. After results are released, speed matters from the applicants' perspective because many applicants receive multiple offers and will often commit to whichever institution completes the admission process more efficiently (Ruffalo Noel Levitz, 2023). The point is that slow admissions are not merely an inconvenience. Instead, they become barriers that prevent admissions from properly guiding applicants toward successful enrollment.

Globally, admission systems have increasingly moved toward innovation. Since the COVID-19 pandemic, institutions have accelerated the adoption of artificial intelligence and cloud platforms to modernize administrative services (EDUCAUSE, 2023; Marinoni et al., 2020). Narrowing the discussion to AI in admissions, the application of AI to admission processes is becoming increasingly relevant. Lira et al. (2023), published in Science Advances, showed that machine learning models can evaluate applicants using unstructured application materials and that these assessments correlate with long-term life outcomes. This strengthens the legitimacy of AI within admissions. Van Busum and Fang (2025) examined bias in AI admission models and raised fairness concerns regarding algorithm-assisted decision-making. Therefore, AI should not completely replace human decision-making but instead function as part of a hybrid admission model where professional judgment remains involved.

With regards to unified admission platforms, Adwan et al. (2023) developed a centralized mobile-based admission application for universities in the GCC region. The system achieved 97.7% usability using Nielsen heuristics, demonstrating that unified admission platforms are practical across institutions. In this case, unified admission pipelines integrate several operational features into one platform to address admission-related bottlenecks. One possible feature is AI-powered chatbots, which can handle routine applicant concerns consistently at scale (Labadze et al., 2023). Another feature is role-based access control, which is important for establishing secure educational information systems operating at institutional scale (Sandhu et al., 1996; Kabier et al., 2023).

In the Philippines, digitalization has largely been driven by policy rather than market pressure. The CHED Strategic Plan (CHED, 2022) establishes institutional direction. Republic Act No. 10931 (2017) increased admission volumes after tuition became free in public higher education institutions, placing additional pressure on institutional staff and infrastructure. Republic Act No. 10173 (2012) imposes the obligation of securely handling applicant records, something paper-based systems cannot fully support. These requirements simultaneously make digitization both necessary and difficult. The National Privacy Commission (2020) has issued advisories on handling applicant records for the education sector. Villarino (2025) found that students in provincial state colleges have already adopted consumer AI tools ahead of institutional frameworks. This creates a governance gap that bypasses proper compliance under Republic Act No. 10173, despite students' readiness and AI literacy. Therefore, admissions should capitalize on this familiarity by channeling students' AI literacy into institutional applications rather than leaving the opportunity unused. AI chatbots are one example of this.

Another relevant point is that every integrated feature within a unified admission pipeline should first be validated independently. Cuerdo et al. (2021) validated EvalBee, an optical mark recognition tool, in a Philippine public school. Dayata and Yap (2024) deployed a retrieval-augmented generation pipeline at the University of San Carlos. Tan (2025) validated K-Means optimization for student course selection at an ACM conference. These independent solutions collectively support admission pipeline development.

Locally, ISPSC Tagudin reflects these national issues. The campus processes approximately 700 to 1,000 freshman applicants annually using a fully manual workflow spanning intake, examination, and release of results. Previous campus studies have attempted to improve institutional processes but not admissions directly. Yukee et al. (2025) validated K-Means profiling on the campus's own entrance exam data. Ballesteros et al. (2025) confirmed the viability of K-Means clustering for student profiling at ISPSC. Eustaquio and Nisperos (2023) built a web-based registration system with decision-support capability for billing, grading, scholarship, and retention tracking. These studies demonstrate that decision-support systems already exist for campus operations. Eustaquio and Nisperos in particular show that a single digital platform can support multiple administrative functions, providing a conceptual basis for an integrated admission platform. However, their system focused primarily on post-admission processes. Angala et al. (2023) implemented a document management system, and Cenezan and Gacusan (2025) built analytics-driven records management. The research gap is discussed in the following paragraph.

The campus does not lack successful digitization initiatives or local research. Instead, it lacks a unified admission testing system. What is needed is a system that is unified, role-governed, and architecturally integrated, bringing together scoring, aptitude assessment, applicant classification, and structured guidance within one pipeline. This integration must comply with Republic Act No. 10173 by securing both physical records and digitally processed applicant information, particularly when data are externally processed (Slade and Prinsloo, 2013). For the AI component, retrieval-augmented generation should rely on institutional policy documents rather than unrestricted large language models to minimize hallucinations (Lewis et al., 2020).

Therefore, a unified role-governed admission testing system is the proposed response to this gap. It integrates automated scoring, predictive applicant profiling, and governed AI advisory services into one admission ecosystem serving the Guidance Office, Registrar, and other institutional stakeholders. The remaining research gap is whether this integrated admission pipeline can outperform the current manual workflow and whether institutional offices will find the system usable and sustainable for long-term implementation.
